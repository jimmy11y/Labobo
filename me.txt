Concept Summary

I am developing an interactive breathing robot intervention to support emotion regulation in children with autism (ages 6 to 12). The robot models slow, rhythmic breathing patterns that the child synchronizes with through visual and tactile engagement. This activates the parasympathetic nervous system, increases heart rate variability, and with repeated sessions builds the child's capacity to downregulate physiological arousal independently. We track change through HRV, fMRI (amygdala-prefrontal connectivity), behavioral observation, and standardized emotion regulation scales.

Most existing breathing-simulating devices are stationary, screen-based, or built for adult use in lab or clinical settings. This project delivers a portable, plush, autism-adapted robot for real-world daily use, targeting a specific clinical population with measurable physiological and neural outcomes. The principle of paced breathing is well established. What is new is translating it into a tool that children with autism can use on their own terms, independently, in their everyday environments.

Research Setup

Multiple-case mixed-methods design with 10 children at Al Shafallah Center in Doha. Each child completes 12 weekly sessions with the robot. Baseline and post-intervention assessments include resting HRV, fMRI at Hamad Medical Corporation, parent-reported emotion regulation questionnaires, and behavioral coding. Interim monitoring occurs at weeks 3, 6, and 9. The team includes clinical advisors, robotics engineers from Qatar University, and neuroscience collaborators.
